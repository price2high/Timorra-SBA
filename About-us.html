Black History Month began in 1926 as "Negro History Week". It was created by Carter G. Woodson, an African American historian, scholar, educator, and publisher. The celebration was timed to coincide with the birthdays of Abraham Lincoln and Frederick Douglass, which fall on February 12 and 14.
The first official observance came in February 1976, from President Gerald Ford whose words established Black History Month in eloquent homage to Woodson and ASALH.


According to W. Marvin Dulaney, the president of the Association for the Study of African American Life and History (ASALH), Woodson was the "father of Black history". Woodson's goal was to educate people about Black history and culture, and to extend and deepen the study of African American history throughout the year.
